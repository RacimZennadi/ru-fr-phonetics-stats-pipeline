How floating-point precision affects scientific conclusions in wav2vec representations

This experiment asked a simple question: if we reduce numerical precision, do we just nudge the numbers a bit, or do we actually change the structure of the representation space enough to change the conclusion? To test this, I extracted wav2vec2 embeddings for repeated word productions from the Russian-French interference corpus and compared cosine-distance statistics after storing the same vectors as float64, float32, float16, and 8-bit quantised values.

The main result is reassuring: in this setup, lower precision mostly perturbed the values rather than breaking the structure. The average intra-speaker distance stayed around 0.3489 and the average inter-speaker distance around 0.4113-0.4117 across all formats. The inter/intra ratio also stayed very stable, at about 1.18 from float64 down to int8. In other words, the numbers moved a little, but not enough to tell a different story. The geometry of the space remained broadly intact, which is exactly what matters for this kind of analysis.

That said, I would not conclude that lower precision is magically harmless just because it behaved nicely here. This experiment was done on a restricted subset of speakers, with mean-pooled word embeddings, cosine distance, and inference only on CPU. That is a fairly calm environment. Training is where the real drama usually begins: gradients can underflow, activations can overflow, and numerical errors can pile up fast. So yes, the numbers were well-behaved here, but they were not being asked to survive a war.

For large-scale speech processing, the answer is therefore nuanced. Float32 is generally safe for both analysis and deployment. Int8 can also be acceptable when the goal is efficient storage or inference and when the task tolerates small distortions. Float16 is more fragile because of its narrow dynamic range: even if it worked reasonably well in this experiment, it is riskier in more sensitive pipelines. In practice, formats like bfloat16 or better-calibrated quantisation schemes are often a better compromise between speed, memory savings, and numerical stability.

So my conclusion is: here, lower precision mainly perturbed the values without fundamentally changing the structure of the representation space. That makes low precision a plausible choice for some kinds of speech analysis and deployment, but only after checking that it does not change the interpretation you care about. Computers are fast, helpful, and deeply unserious about real numbers, so a little caution is still a good idea.
