How floating-point precision affects scientific conclusions in wav2vec representations

This experiment asked whether reducing numerical precision merely perturbs the values of speech representations or whether it fundamentally changes the structure of the representation space. Using wav2vec2 embeddings extracted from repeated productions of the same words in the Russian-French interference corpus, I compared cosine-distance statistics computed from the same vectors stored as float64, float32, float16, and 8-bit quantised values.

In this run, lower precision did not fundamentally alter the scientific conclusion. The average intra-speaker and inter-speaker distances changed only slightly across formats, and their ordering was preserved. The inter/intra ratio remained essentially stable at about 1.18 from float64 down to int8. This suggests that, for this dataset and this analysis protocol, reducing precision mainly perturbed the numerical values rather than destroying the geometry that supports the linguistic interpretation. The mean absolute error stayed extremely small for float32, modest for float16, and still limited for the simple 8-bit quantisation used here.

That said, this result should be interpreted cautiously. Stability in one experiment does not guarantee stability in all settings. The present pipeline uses inference only, mean-pooled word embeddings, cosine distance, a restricted subset of speakers, and CPU-based computation. In more demanding settings, especially during training, low precision can interact with gradient accumulation, normalisation, and extreme activation values in ways that produce overflow, underflow, or catastrophic cancellation. A representation space can therefore appear stable in one analysis while being much more fragile in another.

For large-scale speech processing, the acceptability of low precision depends on the goal. For offline analysis, speaker comparison, indexing, or deployment-oriented inference, lower precision can be very attractive because it reduces storage cost and can accelerate computation while keeping the broad distance structure usable. In that sense, float32 is usually safe, and carefully designed int8 quantisation may also be acceptable when small distortions do not change the qualitative interpretation. By contrast, float16 is more delicate: even if it behaved reasonably here, its limited dynamic range makes it riskier for general use, especially outside controlled inference pipelines. In production systems, bfloat16 or calibrated quantisation schemes are often preferable because they better balance efficiency and numerical robustness.

My conclusion is therefore nuanced: in this experiment, lower precision mainly perturbed the values without fundamentally changing the structure of the representation space. However, low precision should not be treated as universally harmless. It is acceptable when the task tolerates small distortions and when the pipeline has been empirically checked, but it must always be evaluated in relation to the scientific question, the hardware, and the numerical sensitivity of the computations involved.
